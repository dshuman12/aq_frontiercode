# Sausage Fennel Pasta

Notes on Sausage Fennel Pasta, accumulated over several cookings.

## What worked

The first time I made this I followed the recipe straight through. It was
fine. Since then I've made a handful of adjustments that meaningfully
improved the result without changing the recipe's spirit. The big one was
to deglaze the pan with a small splash of vinegar before the wine - the
acidity gives the fond something to hold onto and the final sauce ends up
sharper without any extra bite.

The second meaningful tweak: salt earlier than feels right. The recipe
calls for salt at the end, but if you season the onions while they sweat
they'll soften noticeably faster and you can skip a few minutes of cook
time. The total salt at the end is the same - it just lands at a better
moment.

## What I'd change next time

- Buy a bigger pot. Mine just barely fits the full batch and I lose a
  bit of liquid every time over the lip.
- Try a different brand of sausage fennel pasta - the one I used was
  a touch flat.
- Double the herb at the end. It cooked off more than I expected.

## Pairings

This goes nicely with a glass of an unoaked white
and crusty bread. A simple green salad with a sharp vinaigrette balances the
richness without competing.

## When to make it

Best on a Saturday afternoon when there's no rush. The flavors deepen a
lot in the last 20 minutes of simmering and the kitchen smells incredible
the whole time. Leftovers reheat surprisingly well - I usually eat them
for two more meals over the next couple days.

## Serving suggestion

Top with a generous grating of cheese and a few drops of really good olive
oil right before serving. The contrast between the warm dish and the cold
oil is half the point.

## Equipment

Nothing fancy. A heavy-bottomed pot, a wooden spoon, and a sharp knife.
A pressure cooker shaves the simmer time in half but the texture isn't
quite as good - I only use it on weeknights when time matters more than
the last 5% of flavor.

## Last cooked

I last cooked this on a quiet Sunday and remembered why I keep coming
back to it. Sometimes the simplest recipes are the ones that hold up.
